Ask a developer in San Francisco what their "stack" looks like and they'll start with a model — GPT, Claude, an open-weights checkpoint they fine-tuned over a weekend. Ask a developer across Africa the same question and the answer almost always starts somewhere else entirely: mobile money.

This isn't a quirk. It's a reflection of how value actually moves through the African economy, and it quietly determines what kind of AI products can exist here at all.

## Infrastructure decides what's possible

Mobile money platforms process a staggering share of GDP across the continent every year. For most people, it isn't a payment option — it *is* money. A loan, a salary, a school fee, a side hustle's revenue: all of it flows through mobile money rails that nearly everyone can reach with a basic phone.

When your customers' financial lives run on one API, that API becomes the foundation of everything you build. An AI crop-diagnostics startup doesn't get to think about model accuracy first. It thinks about how a smallholder farmer pays two shillings via M-Pesa in Kenya or MTN MoMo in Ghana to send a photo, and how a payout reaches them when the harvest comes in. The model is the easy part. The rails are the moat.

## The LLM is a feature, not the product

There's a temptation, watching the global AI conversation, to assume the model is the product. Across Africa, the model is increasingly a commodity — you can call a frontier API from anywhere, and open-weights options keep getting better. What you can't easily replicate is distribution that reaches millions of people who don't have credit cards, stable internet, or app-store habits.

That's why the smartest builders here treat the LLM as the last layer they add, not the first. They start with the payment, the USSD fallback, the WhatsApp entry point — the boring plumbing that lets a real person in a real village complete a real transaction. Then they sprinkle intelligence on top.

## Building from the ground floor

This bottom-up instinct produces a different kind of company. It's less likely to demo well at a Demo Day and more likely to survive contact with the market. It assumes constraints — patchy connectivity, low margins, cash-sensitive users — as the starting conditions rather than edge cases to handle later.

The lesson travels beyond any single country. Everywhere, the durable AI businesses will be the ones wired into the infrastructure people already trust. Across Africa, that infrastructure takes many forms — M-Pesa, MTN MoMo, Orange Money, and many others — but the principle is the same: developers learned to respect these rails long before the LLM era arrived.

Build on the rails first. The intelligence can wait.
